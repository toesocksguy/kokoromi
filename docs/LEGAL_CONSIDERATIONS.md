# Legal Considerations: Kokoromi

## Overview

This document addresses potential legal issues when building an Android app based on Anne-Laure Le Cunff's "Tiny Experiments" framework and philosophy.

**Executive Summary**: You can legally build this app. Copyright and trademark law explicitly allow implementing ideas from books. You just can't copy the book's text or confuse users about the framework's origin.

## The Core Legal Principle

**Ideas and concepts are NOT copyrightable—only their specific expression is.**

### What Anne-Laure Le Cunff CANNOT Copyright

- The concept of "tiny experiments" as a philosophy
- The idea of hypothesis-based habit tracking
- The Plus-Minus-Next reflection framework
- The P.A.R.T. (Pact, Act, React, Impact) model
- General methodologies and approaches

### What She CAN Copyright

- The exact wording from her book
- Specific diagrams, illustrations, and examples
- The book's literary expression itself
- Case studies (as written)
- Any original visual designs

---

## What You CAN Legally Do ✅

### 1. Implement the Framework

The Plus-Minus-Next reflection, hypothesis-based experiments, duration-based testing, decision points at the end—all of these are ideas/methodologies, not copyrightable material.

You can fully implement Le Cunff's framework without legal concern.

### 2. Use the Terminology

"Tiny experiments," "pact," "hypothesis," "plus-minus-next" are all open terminology. None are trademarked as of the knowledge cutoff. (Verify via USPTO before launch.)

### 3. Build Your Own App

Many apps exist that implement concepts from books. For example, countless habit trackers implement James Clear's "Atomic Habits" framework. Yours can implement Le Cunff's.

### 4. Give Her Credit

In your app's About section, README, and documentation, acknowledge Le Cunff and her book as inspiration. This is both legal best practice and ethical.

### 5. Create Your Own Brand

Choose a distinct app name and branding. This protects you legally and helps your app stand out.

---

## What You CANNOT Do ⚠️

### 1. Copy Exact Text from the Book

You cannot paste passages from "Tiny Experiments" into your app without permission. This is copyright infringement.

### 2. Use Her Copyrighted Examples

If you use her specific case studies or examples word-for-word, that violates copyright.

### 3. Copy Her Illustrations or Diagrams

Her visual works are copyrighted. You must create your own.

### 4. Trademark "Tiny Experiments"

That name is associated with her book and business (Ness Labs). If it's trademarked (verify USPTO), you cannot use it as your primary app name.

### 5. Claim Her Framework as Your Own

You must attribute the philosophy to her. Failing to do so is unethical and potentially illegal under unfair competition law.

## Trademark Considerations

### "Tiny Experiments" Trademark Status

**What to do**:
1. Search the USPTO database at https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks to check if "Tiny Experiments" is trademarked
2. Search for "Ness Labs" trademark status as well

**If "Tiny Experiments" IS Trademarked**:
- You cannot use "Tiny Experiments" as your primary app name in a way that confuses users
- You COULD name your app something like "Hypothesis," "Pact," or "Curiosity" and reference Le Cunff's framework in your app description
- This is actually better for differentiation anyway

**If "Tiny Experiments" ISN'T Trademarked**:
- You could technically use the name, but it's better practice to create your own brand
- Creating your own brand allows you to protect YOUR app's trademark long-term

### Recommended App Names

Instead of "Kokoromi," consider:
- **Hypothesis** — Emphasizes the testing mindset
- **Pact** — References the commitment aspect
- **Curiosity** — Captures the philosophical core
- **Experimentation Lab** — Descriptive and distinct
- **Test & Learn** — Clear and action-oriented
- Something entirely original — Best for brand protection

## Best Practices to Stay Legal and Ethical

### 1. Give Clear Attribution

**In your app**:
- About section: "This app is inspired by Anne-Laure Le Cunff's framework from *Tiny Experiments: How to Live Freely in a Goal-Obsessed World*"
- Settings/About screen: Link to the book or Ness Labs website

**In your documentation**:
- README.md: Credit the book
- GitHub repo: Include the book reference
- LICENSE: Mention the philosophical inspiration

### 2. Use Your Own Words

- Don't copy definitions or descriptions from the book
- Write your own explanations of how to use Plus-Minus-Next
- Write your own guide on forming a hypothesis
- Create your own examples (you can be inspired by hers, but rewrite)

### 3. Pick Your Own App Name

This protects you from trademark issues and makes your app distinct in the marketplace.

### 4. Feature Her Framework Openly

- Promote the Plus-Minus-Next framework prominently (it's an idea, not owned IP)
- Reference the P.A.R.T. model by name and attribute it
- Implement hypothesis-based experimentation (it's a methodology)
- Explain it's based on Le Cunff's work

### 5. Don't Include Book Text

- ❌ Don't copy chapter excerpts as help text
- ❌ Don't include her case studies verbatim
- ❌ Don't use her specific examples word-for-word
- ✅ You CAN be inspired by examples and create your own versions

## What Could Trigger Legal Action?

Anne-Laure Le Cunff or her publisher (Penguin Random House) would likely only take action if:

1. **You named your app "Tiny Experiments"** and positioned it as the official companion app (trademark/brand confusion)
2. **You copied substantial text from the book** into your app as help content or instructions
3. **You infringed on Ness Labs' trademarks or branding** directly

### They Would NOT Take Action If:

- You implement her philosophy in your own way
- You credit the framework to her
- You use your own distinct app name
- You explain concepts in your own words
- You build an open-source app inspired by her work

## Potential Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Lawsuit Risk (Very Low)

**When**: You name your app "Tiny Experiments" and market it as an official companion app, copying substantial text from the book.

**Outcome**: Cease-and-desist letter from Penguin Random House. You'd be forced to rename and remove infringing content.

**How to Avoid**: Use your own name, use your own words, give clear attribution.

### Scenario 2: Trademark Issue (Low)

**When**: You choose an app name that's already trademarked (e.g., another app called "Hypothesis").

**Outcome**: You'd be asked to rename before publishing.

**How to Avoid**: Search USPTO before naming your app.

### Scenario 3: Positive Outcome (Likely)

**When**: You build a high-quality open-source app, give clear attribution, use your own words, and credit Le Cunff.

**Outcome**: Anne-Laure might appreciate it, potentially share it, maybe even collaborate or endorse it.

**How to Achieve**: Follow the best practices above.

## Bottom Line

**You have legal permission to build this app.** Copyright and trademark law explicitly allow implementing ideas from books. Thousands of apps implement frameworks from published books.

Your app is a different implementation of her philosophy—just like how hundreds of habit trackers exist even though they're all inspired by James Clear's "Atomic Habits" or BJ Fogg's "Tiny Habits."
